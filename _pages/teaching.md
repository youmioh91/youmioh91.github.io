## Overview

I am committed to training the next generation of Earth system scientists through
research mentoring, interdisciplinary education, and hands-on engagement with
observations, models, and data.

My teaching and mentoring span atmospheric science, environmental science,
Earth system modeling, methane biogeochemistry, inverse modeling, stable isotopes,
and artificial intelligence. Across these areas, I aim to help students become
curious, independent, and confident scientists who can connect fundamental
concepts with consequential environmental problems.

My educational approach is organized around three goals:

1. connecting scientific concepts to real-world environmental challenges;
2. developing critical and independent thinking through inquiry-based learning; and
3. building the technical depth needed for students to become experts in their fields.

## Teaching Philosophy

### Connecting science to the real world

Students engage more deeply when they can see how scientific concepts relate to
the world around them. I therefore use current environmental observations, news,
visualizations, and publicly available datasets to connect classroom concepts with
questions about climate change, atmospheric composition, air quality, extreme
events, and greenhouse-gas mitigation.

For example, students learning about the global methane cycle can examine observed
changes in atmospheric methane, explore how emissions and atmospheric removal
contribute to those changes, and evaluate the evidence supporting different
scientific interpretations.

I also use interactive visualization to make complex Earth system processes more
accessible. Through the CarbonTracker-CH₄ project, I advised the development of an
interactive tool that allows users to explore global methane distributions,
atmospheric transport, and surface emissions.

[Explore the CarbonTracker-CH₄ visualization](https://gml.noaa.gov/ccgg/carbontracker-ch4/visualization.html){:target="_blank"}

I am also working with NOAA's
[Science On a Sphere](https://sos.noaa.gov/){:target="_blank"}
program to communicate CarbonTracker-CH₄ results through an immersive,
museum-based visualization platform.

### Developing scientific thinking through inquiry

My teaching emphasizes inquiry rather than memorization. I encourage students to
begin with a scientific question, identify their assumptions, examine relevant
evidence, and develop a defensible interpretation.

When introducing the global methane budget, for example, I first ask students how
they expect atmospheric methane to change over time. Students then use
observational evidence to divide the methane budget into sources and sinks,
evaluate competing explanations, and communicate their reasoning.

In upper-level and graduate education, I incorporate:

- discussion of recent scientific literature;
- evaluation of hypotheses, methods, uncertainties, and limitations;
- collaborative analysis of observational and model data;
- reproducible computational workflows; and
- open-ended research projects.

These activities help students understand that scientific knowledge is developed
through an iterative process of questioning, analysis, discussion, and revision.

### Building technical depth

Once students have developed curiosity and scientific reasoning skills, I help
them build the disciplinary and computational depth required to address complex
Earth system questions.

My interdisciplinary training in environmental engineering, biology, atmospheric
science, and Earth system modeling allows me to connect physical, chemical,
biological, and data-driven perspectives.

In computational courses, I would have students work directly with real datasets
and construct simplified models of Earth system processes. Rather than treating a
model as a black box, students would examine its assumptions, test its sensitivity,
quantify uncertainty, and compare its output with observations.

Tools and datasets may include:

- CarbonTracker-CH₄;
- atmospheric greenhouse-gas observations;
- satellite remote-sensing products;
- process-based Earth system models;
- inverse modeling systems;
- stable-isotope measurements; and
- knowledge-guided machine-learning tools such as PyKGML.

## Mentoring Philosophy

I meet students where they are by recognizing their existing knowledge,
experiences, motivations, and professional goals. Early in a project, I provide
structured guidance and help students translate broad interests into tractable
scientific questions. As their confidence and expertise grow, I gradually shift
decision-making responsibility to them.

I aim to create a research-group culture in which students feel comfortable asking
questions, proposing new ideas, acknowledging uncertainty, and learning from
unsuccessful approaches. Regular individual and group meetings provide both
technical guidance and opportunities for peer learning.

As an international Asian woman in science, I also recognize the importance of
visible mentorship and belonging. I strive to help students from different
backgrounds see themselves as valued members and future leaders of the scientific
community.

## Guest Lectures and Research Education

I regularly communicate current methane and Earth system research through invited
lectures, seminars, student workshops, and interdisciplinary working-group
activities.

Lecture topics include:

- the global methane cycle;
- recent changes in atmospheric methane;
- methane sources, sinks, and atmospheric lifetime;
- stable isotopes and greenhouse-gas source attribution;
- CarbonTracker-CH₄ and atmospheric inverse modeling;
- microbial methane oxidation;
- wetland methane emissions;
- process-based Earth system modeling; and
- artificial intelligence for environmental science.

My guest lectures connect foundational concepts with current research and introduce
students to the observations, computational tools, and unresolved questions that
shape modern Earth system science.

## Educational Goals

As a faculty member, I would integrate teaching, mentoring, and research rather
than treating them as separate activities. Students would engage with real
observations, current scientific questions, and accessible research tools from the
beginning of their education.

My goal is for students to leave my courses and research group able to:

- formulate meaningful scientific questions;
- evaluate evidence critically;
- work confidently with environmental datasets and models;
- understand the assumptions and limitations of scientific tools;
- collaborate across disciplinary boundaries;
- communicate their findings clearly; and
- see themselves as capable contributors to Earth and environmental science.
