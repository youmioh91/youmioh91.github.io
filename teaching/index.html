Teaching & Mentoring

Overview

I am committed to training the next generation of Earth system scientists through research mentoring, interdisciplinary education, and hands-on engagement with observations, models, and data.

My teaching and mentoring span atmospheric science, environmental science, Earth system modeling, methane biogeochemistry, inverse modeling, stable isotopes, and artificial intelligence. Across these areas, I aim to help students become curious, independent, and confident scientists who can connect fundamental concepts with consequential environmental problems.

My educational approach is organized around three goals:

  1. connecting scientific concepts to real-world environmental challenges;
  2. developing critical and independent thinking through inquiry-based learning; and
  3. building the technical depth needed for students to become experts in their fields.

Teaching Philosophy

Connecting science to the real world

Students engage more deeply when they can see how scientific concepts relate to the world around them. I therefore use current environmental observations, news, visualizations, and publicly available datasets to connect classroom concepts with questions about climate change, atmospheric composition, air quality, extreme events, and greenhouse-gas mitigation.

For example, students learning about the global methane cycle can examine observed changes in atmospheric methane, explore how emissions and atmospheric removal contribute to those changes, and evaluate the evidence supporting different scientific interpretations.

I also use interactive visualization to make complex Earth system processes more accessible. Through the CarbonTracker-CH₄ project, I advised the development of an interactive tool that allows users to explore global methane distributions, atmospheric transport, and surface emissions.

Explore the CarbonTracker-CH₄ visualization

I am also working with NOAA’s Science On a Sphere program to communicate CarbonTracker-CH₄ results through an immersive, museum-based visualization platform.

Developing scientific thinking through inquiry

My teaching emphasizes inquiry rather than memorization. I encourage students to begin with a scientific question, identify their assumptions, examine relevant evidence, and develop a defensible interpretation.

When introducing the global methane budget, for example, I first ask students how they expect atmospheric methane to change over time. Students then use observational evidence to divide the methane budget into sources and sinks, evaluate competing explanations, and communicate their reasoning.

In upper-level and graduate education, I incorporate:

  • discussion of recent scientific literature;
  • evaluation of hypotheses, methods, uncertainties, and limitations;
  • collaborative analysis of observational and model data;
  • reproducible computational workflows; and
  • open-ended research projects.

These activities help students understand that scientific knowledge is developed through an iterative process of questioning, analysis, discussion, and revision.

Building technical depth

Once students have developed curiosity and scientific reasoning skills, I help them build the disciplinary and computational depth required to address complex Earth system questions.

My interdisciplinary training in environmental engineering, biology, atmospheric science, and Earth system modeling allows me to connect physical, chemical, biological, and data-driven perspectives.

In computational courses, I would have students work directly with real datasets and construct simplified models of Earth system processes. Rather than treating a model as a black box, students would examine its assumptions, test its sensitivity, quantify uncertainty, and compare its output with observations.

Tools and datasets may include:

  • CarbonTracker-CH₄;
  • atmospheric greenhouse-gas observations;
  • satellite remote-sensing products;
  • process-based Earth system models;
  • inverse modeling systems;
  • stable-isotope measurements; and
  • knowledge-guided machine-learning tools such as PyKGML.

Research Mentoring

Research mentoring is a central part of my educational work. I have mentored postdoctoral researchers, graduate students, and undergraduate researchers working across atmospheric science, ecology, Earth system modeling, and artificial intelligence.

My mentoring approach emphasizes:

  • clearly defined scientific questions;
  • regular individual and group meetings;
  • increasing independence as projects progress;
  • reproducible coding and data-management practices;
  • scientific writing and presentation skills;
  • collaboration across disciplinary backgrounds; and
  • a supportive research environment grounded in belonging and open communication.

Mentoring Philosophy

I meet students where they are by recognizing their existing knowledge, experiences, motivations, and professional goals. Early in a project, I provide structured guidance and help students translate broad interests into tractable scientific questions. As their confidence and expertise grow, I gradually shift decision-making responsibility to them.

I aim to create a research-group culture in which students feel comfortable asking questions, proposing new ideas, acknowledging uncertainty, and learning from unsuccessful approaches. Regular individual and group meetings provide both technical guidance and opportunities for peer learning.

As an international Asian woman in science, I also recognize the importance of visible mentorship and belonging. I strive to help students from different backgrounds see themselves as valued members and future leaders of the scientific community.

Guest Lectures and Research Education

I regularly communicate current methane and Earth system research through invited lectures, seminars, student workshops, and interdisciplinary working-group activities.

Lecture topics include:

  • the global methane cycle;
  • recent changes in atmospheric methane;
  • methane sources, sinks, and atmospheric lifetime;
  • stable isotopes and greenhouse-gas source attribution;
  • CarbonTracker-CH₄ and atmospheric inverse modeling;
  • microbial methane oxidation;
  • wetland methane emissions;
  • process-based Earth system modeling; and
  • artificial intelligence for environmental science.

My guest lectures connect foundational concepts with current research and introduce students to the observations, computational tools, and unresolved questions that shape modern Earth system science.

Educational and Community Leadership

My educational contributions extend beyond individual mentoring and classroom teaching. I help create scientific communities in which students and early-career researchers can learn, collaborate, and develop professional networks.

AI4Methane

As Principal Investigator of the AI4Methane Working Group, I coordinate an interdisciplinary community connecting environmental scientists, modelers, and artificial-intelligence researchers. The group supports collaborative research, student participation, workshops, shared datasets, and open-source tools for methane science.

NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory Seminar Series

As an organizer of the NOAA GML Seminar Series, I help bring researchers and students together to discuss emerging topics in atmospheric composition, climate, observations, and modeling.

CIRES Mentoring Program

Through the CIRES Mentoring Program, I contribute to efforts that support professional development, career guidance, and an inclusive research environment.

Workshops and Scientific Sessions

I have organized interdisciplinary workshops and scientific sessions for the American Geophysical Union and other research communities. These activities create opportunities for students and early-career scientists to present their work, receive feedback, and build collaborations.

Educational Goals

As a faculty member, I would integrate teaching, mentoring, and research rather than treating them as separate activities. Students would engage with real observations, current scientific questions, and accessible research tools from the beginning of their education.

My goal is for students to leave my courses and research group able to:

  • formulate meaningful scientific questions;
  • evaluate evidence critically;
  • work confidently with environmental datasets and models;
  • understand the assumptions and limitations of scientific tools;
  • collaborate across disciplinary boundaries;
  • communicate their findings clearly; and
  • see themselves as capable contributors to Earth and environmental science.
